Brief description on Ghost of Tsushima

Sucker Punch Productions created the action-adventure game Ghost of Tsushima, which Sony Interactive Entertainment released. The player takes control of Jin Sakai, a samurai on a mission to defend Tsushima Island, in this game, which is set in 1274 during the first Mongol invasion of Japan. There is a sizable open area in the game that may be explored on foot or on horseback. When confronted by foes, the player has two options: use Jin's katana to fight them head-on or transform into "The Ghost," a famous fighter who kills opponents covertly.

Stance Gameplay

Although the fundamentals of fighting are simple, not every opponent you encounter will be amenable to your novice sword stance. Along the way in the game, you'll encounter different foes who bring more than just swords to the fight. You must master and use new sword postures in order to defeat them.

Water

Water stance can break through shields. The Water Stances held heavy attack is a flurry of strikes that deal burst damage to enemies with shields. Its piercing attack is also very powerful and rapidly closes the gap between Jin and his enemies.

Wind

Wind stance can pierce the defense of spear users. The Wind Stances held heavy attack is a series of whirls and kicks that deal burst damage to enemies with polearms. Its piercing attack is also very powerful and rapidly closes the gap between Jin and his enemies.

Moon

Moon stance is effective against brutes. The Moon Stances held heavy attack is a series of powerful strikes that deal bonus stagger damage to brute enemies. Its piercing attack is also very powerful and rapidly closes the gap between Jin and his enemies.

Landscape

The carefully designed gaming environment captures the variety of life on the large island, from vast plains and serene shrines to historic forests, hamlets, and imposing mountains. The game's landscape is replete with vivid, lovely moments that are atmospheric, memorable, and stunning settings in both new and classic video game universes.
